I'm really happy to share that our work with [Bevar Ukraine](https://bevarukraine.dk/) and AWS has been published as an official [AWS success story](https://aws.amazon.com/solutions/case-studies/bevar-ukraine-case-study/).

It's a strange and good feeling to see a project you started as a volunteer end up written up by AWS. But the part I keep coming back to isn't the write-up. It's the people on the other side of the screen.

## What ViktorAI is

Together with a great volunteer team, we built **ViktorAI** — an AI-powered assistant that helps Ukrainian refugees in Denmark with the everyday questions that come with starting over in a new country.

Not abstract questions. Real ones. How does the Danish legal system work? Where do I go for healthcare? How do I get my kids into school? What about social services, jobs, housing, residency? The kind of thing that, before ViktorAI, often meant waiting days or weeks to reach a social worker who was already stretched thin.

Now, if you have a simple question, you can get an answer right away — 24/7, in your own language.

## It started as a volunteer project

This wasn't a product with a roadmap and a budget. It started as people trying to help, and it grew because the need was real and the need was constant.

Today ViktorAI has:

- helped **10,000+ people**
- supports **4,000+ monthly active users**
- and has answered **100,000+ questions**

Under the hood it's a network of specialized agents running on Amazon Bedrock — translation, legal, healthcare, and an orchestrator that routes each question to the right place. But honestly, the architecture is the least interesting part of this story to me.

## Why this one matters to me

For me, this project is a good reminder that tech can be *very* human when it's built around real needs instead of around how clever the demo looks.

When the person on the other side is already stressed, already navigating a new system in a new language, the work changes. It can't just be "cool." It has to be clear, reliable, respectful, and actually useful. There's no room for a clever answer that's subtly wrong. They don't need a demo. They need an answer.

That experience is a big part of why I care so much about human-centered AI now. AI shouldn't automate something just because it technically can. It should help people do meaningful things they couldn't do as easily before.
