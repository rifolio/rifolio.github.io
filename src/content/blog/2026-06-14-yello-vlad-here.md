Hi. I'm Vladyslav Horbatenko, but nobody really calls me that. It's just Vlad.

I build AI systems for a living, I'm a co-founder, and I'm currently doing my MSc in Human-Centered Artificial Intelligence at DTU. What I'm drawn to hasn't really changed since I started: things that are technically interesting, actually useful to real people, and just hard enough at the start that I'm not sure I can pull them off. That little bit of "wait, can I actually do this?" is usually how I know a project is worth my time.

These days most of my energy goes into [PeerMind](https://peermind.de/), where I'm CTO. We're building AI tools for education, and honestly I do a bit of everything. I build a lot, I iterate, I argue things out with the team, I'm deep in the IT and the architecture one day and the business side the next, I try out new frameworks, and I'm changing and improving things basically all the time. Part of that is thinking about how people actually learn: how a student gets stuck, how a teacher puts a lesson together, how a model can read a document and not completely miss the point. But it's also just trying to make the thing genuinely good. Good enough that I'd personally enjoy using it. I find all of it fascinating, not only as a product problem but as a human one.

On the side I take on consulting and freelance work when something interesting comes along, usually messy AI projects where nobody has figured out the "right" way to do it yet. Those are my favorite. No clean recipe, a bit of research, a bit of engineering, a bit of guessing, a bit of chaos.

I also work with [Tryp.com](https://tryp.com/) on AI for travel, mostly around customer support and internal tooling. I work on automation and optimization, and on internal tools that help the people doing customer support do their job better. Lately I took on a whole project on my own: building a voice AI call center from scratch. Travel turned out to be one of the more humbling spaces to build in. So many prices, preferences, edge cases, and just plain messy real-world data. You quickly learn it's never "ask an LLM and you're done." You have to build something that reasons, double-checks itself, and actually helps the person on the other end.

## I learn by building

I've made my peace with the fact that I'm not great at learning things in the abstract. I learn by building them.

Being a co-founder made that even more obvious. You don't get to stay in one lane. One day I'm deep in backend logic, the next I'm worrying about users, deployment, architecture, pricing, a bug that makes no sense, or why something that felt completely obvious in my head lands as confusing to someone else.

Honestly? That's the part I love.

I like sitting just slightly outside my comfort zone: not lost, but uncomfortable enough that I have to learn quickly. That's where almost all of my real growth has come from. And building has taught me that shipping features is only half of it. The rest is getting better at asking the right questions, picking trade-offs I can live with, listening to users, and accepting, every single time, that the first version of anything I make is probably wrong.

## DSPy, LangGraph, and the fun technical stuff

I try to stay close to what's actually happening in AI, not the headlines, but the tools. I like trying them, building with them, breaking them at work, comparing them, and seeing what still stands after real use.

For a long while that tool was DSPy, and I still have a soft spot for it. It made building with LLMs feel scientific instead of like writing magic incantations. You define modules, optimize behavior, evaluate outputs, and it reminded me of the way I think when I'm closer to PyTorch and actual ML. I leaned on it a lot, in my studies and at work, for prompt optimization, retrieval, multi-step reasoning, that kind of thing. It quietly changed how I reason about LLM systems.

Lately though, I've been drifting toward LangGraph, and the reason is simple: control. As the systems I build get more complicated, I want my hands on the state, the flows, the agents, the retries, the fallbacks, how all the pieces talk. LangGraph gives me that room. I still borrow ideas from DSPy, but more and more I just rebuild the parts I need myself, shaped around the actual problem.

That's pretty much my whole approach in a sentence: learn from the frameworks, take what's good, and rebuild the rest to fit.

Day to day I'm usually somewhere around LLMs, agents, RAG, vector databases, document processing, evaluation, LangChain, LangGraph, DSPy, Pinecone, ChromaDB, Hugging Face, PyTorch, TensorFlow, Django, Next.js, PostgreSQL, Docker, AWS, Azure, and GCP. The list changes constantly. The part that matters is learning how to *think* with the tools, not memorizing this week's stack.

## Building for people who actually need it

Some of the work that's stuck with me most has been NGO and social-impact work.

I helped build AI-powered support systems connected to [Bevar Ukraine](https://bevarukraine.dk/), making tools that help Ukrainians in Denmark find information, guidance, and services. We even wrote about it on the AWS Machine Learning Blog: [Build a scalable AI assistant to help refugees using AWS](https://aws.amazon.com/blogs/machine-learning/build-a-scalable-ai-assistant-to-help-refugees-using-aws/). This one was never an abstract problem for me. It mattered, personally.

That kind of work changes how you build. It can't just be "cool." It has to be clear, reliable, respectful, and genuinely useful, because there's a real person on the other side who's already stressed and doesn't need a clever demo. They need an answer. That experience is a big part of why I care so much about human-centered AI now. AI shouldn't automate something just because it technically can. It should help people do meaningful work better.

## RUC, DTU, and the academia side

Before DTU I studied Computer Science and Mathematics at Roskilde University.

RUC is open in the best way. You get to choose your own projects and shape your own direction, and I took as much of that freedom as I could. I used it to go deep into math, programming, machine learning, and project work, and I loved that I could connect the theory to actually building something instead of treating them as separate worlds.

The thing I'm probably most proud of from that time: I pushed to do my bachelor project together with [Novo Nordisk](https://www.novonordisk.com/). That's not exactly the standard move at bachelor level at RUC, and making it happen meant a lot to me.

Now at DTU I'm doing Human-Centered Artificial Intelligence, which honestly feels like it was written for the version of me that already existed: technically serious AI, but built to be useful, understandable, and designed around people.

I like living in the gap between academia and startups. Academia gives me depth and the space to actually question things; startups give me speed, pressure, and a constant reality check. Put together, they keep me honest.

## What I actually care about

I care about useful AI. Not AI for slides, not AI for hype, not the kind that sounds impressive in a pitch and falls apart the second a real user touches it.

I like AI that helps a student learn, helps a teacher create, helps a support team do better work, helps someone find an answer, helps a small org punch above its weight, helps a builder build faster.

And I care a lot about learning itself, about being around curious, ambitious people who aren't afraid to admit they're still figuring it out. Because I definitely am too. That's not a disclaimer; it's kind of the whole point. It's what keeps this fun for me.
